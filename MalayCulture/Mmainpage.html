Let's Learn About the History of the Malays in Malaysia.


Malaysian Malays (Malay: Orang Melayu Malaysia, Jawi: ملايو مليسيا) are Malaysians of Malay ethnicity whose ancestry originates wholly or partly in the Malay world. According to the 2023 population estimate, with a total population of 17.6 million, Malaysian Malays form 57.9% of Malaysia's demographics, the largest ethnic group in the country. They can be broadly classified into two main categories; Anak Jati (indigenous Malays or local Malays) and Anak Dagang (trading Malays or foreign Malays).

The Anak Jati or native Malays consist of those individuals who adhere to the Malay culture native to the coastal areas of Malay Peninsula and Borneo.[3] Among notable groups include the Bruneians, Kedahans, Kelantanese, Pahangite, Perakians, Sarawakians and Terengganuans. On the other hand, the Anak Dagang or foreign Malays, consist of descendants of immigrants from other parts of Malay Archipelago who became the citizens of the Malay sultanates and were absorbed and assimilated into Malay culture at different times, aided by similarity in lifestyle and common religion.

The foreign Malays have Acehnese, Banjarese, Buginese, Javanese, Mandailing and Minangkabau ancestries that come from Indonesia.[4][5] Some foreign Malays may also come from other parts of Southeast Asia, that includes the Chams of Indochina, Cocos Malays of Australian Cocos (Keeling) Islands as well as the Patani Malays of southern Thailand. There is also a minority of Malays who are partially descended from more recent immigrants from many other countries who have assimilated into Malay Muslim culture.

Religions
  • Islam plays a pivotal role in the lives of Malaysian Malays. The majority of Malays are Sunni Muslims, and Islam is not only a religion but also a significant element of their cultural identity. The arrival of Islam in the 15th century profoundly transformed Malay society. The Sultanate of Malacca was instrumental in spreading Islam across the Malay Peninsula, which became a dominant force shaping the social and cultural landscape of the Malays.
  • Islamic practices and principles influence various aspects of daily life for Malaysian Malays. Religious observances such as daily prayers, fasting during Ramadan, and the performance of Hajj are integral to their spiritual lives. Islamic law (Sharia) is also incorporated into the legal system in Malaysia, particularly in matters of personal status such as marriage, inheritance, and family law.
  • In addition to religious practices, Islamic education plays a crucial role. Many Malays attend religious schools (Sekolah Agama) alongside secular schools, where they learn about Islamic teachings, Quranic studies, and Arabic language. This dual approach to education helps preserve and promote Islamic values within the community while also equipping individuals with the skills needed for broader societal participation.
Education
  • Education is highly valued among Malaysian Malays, with a strong emphasis on both academic achievement and Islamic learning. The Malaysian education system comprises public and private institutions that offer a range of educational opportunities from primary to tertiary levels.
  • Malaysian Malays typically attend public schools where the curriculum is based on the national education system. These schools offer instruction in various subjects, including mathematics, science, languages, and social studies, following a syllabus set by the Ministry of Education. Many Malays also attend religious schools to receive Islamic education, where they study Islamic history, jurisprudence, and the Quran.
  • For higher education, Malaysian Malays have access to universities and colleges across Malaysia and abroad. Public universities, such as Universiti Malaya and Universiti Putra Malaysia, offer a wide range of undergraduate and postgraduate programs. Additionally, there are institutions specializing in Islamic studies, such as the International Islamic University Malaysia (IIUM), which cater to those seeking advanced religious education.
  • The Malaysian government implements various policies to ensure educational access and equity among different ethnic groups. Programs like the Bumiputera policy provide opportunities and support for Malays and other indigenous groups to advance in education and employment. This policy aims to address socio-economic disparities and promote balanced development within the country.
